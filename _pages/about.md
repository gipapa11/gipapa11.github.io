I am a development economist and Postdoctoral Fellow at Georgetown University. My research spans education, labor markets, property rights, climate, environment, and conflict displacement, with fieldwork concentrated in Kenya and East Africa.

My research on property rights under climate change begins with theory. In *Groundless Claims: Property Rights When Land Ceases to Exist*, I extend the Besley (1995) framework to settings where environmental destruction permanently eliminates land, calibrated to Kenya's rising Rift Valley lakes — where expanding lakes have displaced more than 80,000 households since 2010.

A second strand tests the theory's predictions empirically. *Property Rights, Climate Displacement, and Household Welfare: Evidence from Kenya's Rising Lakes* combines satellite-derived measures of lake expansion (2005–2025) with a planned household survey of displaced households across formal, customary, and informal tenure systems, asking how tenure status shapes compensation, adaptation, and recovery.

My job market paper, *The Hidden Costs of Recycling: Lead Exposure and Student Learning*, estimates the causal effect of battery-recycling lead exposure on children's learning in Kenya using national administrative test data, finding effects that rival the gains from leading school-based interventions.

I am also a co-investigator on *Saturating the Safety Net*, a randomized saturation-design RCT covering roughly 4,000 refugee and host households in urban Nairobi (with Andrew Zeitlin, Billy Jack, and A. Wendo), studying cash transfers, skills training, and their spillovers in an urban refugee economy.

I received my Ph.D. in Economics from the University of Delaware in 2024. Before graduate school, I spent eight years at Innovations for Poverty Action in Kenya managing large impact evaluations, and I have consulted for the World Bank since 2018.
