I am a development economist and Postdoctoral Fellow at Georgetown University. I study the environmental, educational, and labor-market determinants of human capital in low- and middle-income countries, using field and natural experiments together with large administrative and survey data. My work examines how early-life environmental exposures such as lead pollution shape children's learning, how cash transfers and skills training affect the livelihoods of refugees and low-income households, and how property rights and adaptation evolve under climate-driven displacement. My research has appeared in the *Review of Economics and Statistics*.

My research on property rights under climate change begins with theory. In [*Groundless Claims: Property Rights When Land Ceases to Exist*](/publication/groundless-claims), I extend the Besley (1995) framework to settings where environmental destruction permanently eliminates land, calibrated to Kenya's Rift Valley, where rising lakes have submerged over 100 km² since 2005 and displaced more than 80,000 households. Formally titled households recover substantially more welfare than informal occupants, and displacement amplifies pre-existing gender inequality.

A second strand tests the theory's predictions empirically. [*Property Rights, Climate Displacement, and Household Welfare: Evidence from Kenya's Rising Lakes*](/publication/rising-lakes) combines satellite-derived measures of lake expansion (2005–2025) with a planned household survey of displaced households across formal, customary, and informal tenure systems, asking how tenure status shapes compensation, adaptation, and recovery.

In [*The Hidden Costs of Recycling: Lead Exposure and Student Learning*](/publication/hidden-costs-recycling), I exploit the 2007 opening of 26 used-lead-acid-battery recycling plants in Kenya as a natural experiment. Using individual national-exam records, I find that recycling-driven lead exposure lowers test scores by about 0.18 standard deviations — rivaling the gains from leading school-based interventions — with heterogeneity by gender and school type.

I am also a co-investigator on [*Saturating the Safety Net*](/publication/saturating-safety-net), a randomized saturation-design RCT among roughly 4,000 refugee and host-community households in urban Nairobi (with Andrew Zeitlin, Billy Jack, and Alex Wendo), measuring the effects of cash and entrepreneurship training on livelihoods, social cohesion, and local prices.

I received my Ph.D. in Economics from the University of Delaware in 2024. Before graduate school, I spent more than a decade leading randomized field experiments with Innovations for Poverty Action in Kenya, and I have consulted for the World Bank since 2018.
